# Cinematic Scenes and Multiline Prose

## Build a visible scene

Establish only the spatial anchors needed for action. Choose a dominant sensory channel, a moving element and a focal object or person. Let the focus change when the emotional meaning changes. Use light, sound, weather, architecture and movement as active pressure, not inventory.

Do not write camera directions. Create a cinematic effect through selection, blocking, timing and sensory continuity.

## Connect spectacle to character

Before a set piece, identify whose belief or relationship it tests. During it, make physical choices reveal character. After it, preserve a cost, changed relationship, new fear or altered understanding. Remove spectacle that leaves everyone unchanged.

## Create emotional contrast

Allow ordinary detail and humor to lower defenses. Introduce unease through a concrete sensory contradiction. Keep the strongest emotion partly displaced into action, object or missed dialogue. Give climaxes an aftermath; do not explain the feeling twice.

## Write dialogue with defenses

Give each speaker an immediate conversational goal and an underlying need they may avoid. Let relationship, status and fear shape what can be said. Avoid turning every exchange into a quotation-ready declaration.

## Execute multiline transitions

Switch only at a meaningful pressure point. Bridge lines through one of:

- matched image or sound;
- cause in one line and effect in another;
- question and delayed answer;
- opposing interpretations of the same event;
- shared countdown or converging movement.

Re-anchor viewpoint, place and immediate objective quickly after a switch. Never import knowledge from the outgoing viewpoint into the incoming one.

## Control prose intensity

Reserve dense imagery and extended rhythm for moments that earn expansion. Use plain, quick language for comic timing, logistics and action clarity. Contrast creates force; continuous grandeur flattens it.
