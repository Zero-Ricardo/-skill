# Multiline Narrative and Tension

## Distinguish viewpoints from lines

A viewpoint is who perceives a scene. A narrative line is a continuing pursuit with its own actor, objective, resistance, information and change. Occasional viewpoint chapters do not by themselves create multiline narrative.

For every arc-level line record:

- carrier and objective;
- initial misconception or information advantage;
- opposing force;
- change at each phase;
- intersections with other lines;
- convergence or deliberate non-resolution.

## Build the sequence matrix

For each chapter record the primary viewpoint, lines advanced, new pressure, information gained or withheld, cost, handoff and convergence function. No important line should disappear accidentally; an absence must create suspense or reflect priority.

## Choose a chapter structure

- single-line continuous;
- dominant line with a short counterpoint;
- alternating parallel lines;
- visual or thematic match cut between locations;
- handoff where one line's consequence becomes another line's problem.

Every switch must change tension, information, contrast or causality. Avoid decorative switching and repeated “meanwhile.”

## Design tension at three levels

- Arc: desire, escalating opposition, cost, breaking misconception and convergence.
- Sequence: pressure must grow or change form; alternate compression and recovery rather than increasing volume continuously.
- Scene: enter with an objective, meet resistance, change tactic, gain something at a cost, and leave in a different state.

Track what readers know that characters do not, what characters conceal from one another, and which expectation a turn revises. End chapters with a changed meaning or emotional residue, not only an unexplained threat.
